In 1951, Emperor Haile Selassie dispatched thousands of soldiers drawn from his elite Imperial Bodyguard to fight alongside American-led UN forces in Korea. They were the Kagnew Battalions — and they became one of the most decorated units of the entire war.

## Who Were the Kagnew?

The name "Kagnew" comes from an Amharic word meaning "to bring order out of chaos." It was also the name of a legendary horse ridden by Emperor Tewodros II. The battalions lived up to that name on the battlefield.

Six thousand Ethiopians crossed the world to fight for the freedom of people they had never met. They suffered 121 killed and 536 wounded in battle. Not a single soldier was ever captured.

## A Record Unmatched

The three Ethiopian battalions fought 253 battles and never lost a man to capture. Their motto: *"Never be captured on the war field."* It was not just a saying — it was a promise kept under fire.

## Why Their Story Matters Today

This was the only time in history that an African nation voluntarily sent its own soldiers to fight outside the continent under a collective security framework. Their valor is extraordinary. Their story is almost entirely unknown outside Ethiopia.

With fewer than 200 survivors still living today, the window to document their testimonies is closing. That is why this film must be made now.
